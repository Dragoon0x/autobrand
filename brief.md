# Brand Brief

> Be specific where you have opinions. Be open where you want the agent to explore.
> The better this brief is, the better the output. Your taste lives here.

## What is this?

**Name:** Thin Air
**One-liner:** A design studio that builds things from nothing.
**Domain:** Creative technology, product design, brand systems

## Who is it for?

Founders who are allergic to corporate design. People who want something that feels built, not bought. Early-stage teams who need a brand that works hard from day one but doesn't look like it came from a template.

They've seen enough gradient logos and geometric sans-serifs to last a lifetime. They want something with a point of view.

## What should it feel like?

- Calm confidence. Not loud, not trying to impress. Just clearly good.
- Slightly editorial. Like a well-designed independent magazine, not a SaaS landing page.
- Warmth without softness. Approachable but not cute.
- The feeling of a well-organized workspace. Everything has a place. Nothing is decorative.

## What should it NEVER feel like?

- Corporate. No blue-and-white, no rounded-corner-everything, no stock photo energy.
- Trendy. Nothing that screams 2024 AI startup aesthetic (dark mode gradients, blurry mesh backgrounds).
- Precious. It should feel like it can get scratched and still look good.
- Noisy. If it's competing for attention, it's wrong.

## Color direction

Lean warm. Think paper, stone, clay. The palette should feel like materials, not pixels. One strong accent color that earns its place. No neon. No pastels.

## Typography direction

Something with character for headlines. Not a geometric sans. Something that has an opinion. Body text should be invisible, just do its job well. Mono or slab for functional elements (captions, metadata, code).

## Voice and tone

Write like a smart person talking to another smart person. No filler. No fluff. No exclamation marks. Sentences that are short when they need to be and longer when the thought demands it. Dry humor is welcome. Corporate jargon is not.

If a tagline sounds like it could be on a billboard, it's probably wrong. If it sounds like something you'd say to a friend, it's probably right.

## References (things that feel right)

- The typography and layout of Monocle magazine
- The restraint of Dieter Rams
- The warmth of a Muji store
- Linear's interface design (clarity without coldness)
- The tone of Craig Mod's writing

## Anti-references (things to avoid)

- Anything that looks like it was made in Canva
- The generic "modern startup" look (Inter font, blue accents, rounded cards)
- Over-designed luxury branding (too much gold, too much serif drama)
- Brutalist web design (interesting as art, wrong for this)

## Hard constraints

- Logo must work at 16x16 favicon size
- All colors must meet WCAG AA contrast ratios
- Palette must work in both light and dark contexts
- No more than 3 typefaces total
- SVG logomark must be under 5KB
